## Methodology — how each Q gets measured

### Q1 — paired same-cell replication

For each of the 3 cells (rmsnorm/fp16/large, rmsnorm/bf16/medium,
silu_mul/fp16/large), the paired-control warmstart emits:

```
ref_0 → novel_0 → ref_1 → novel_1 → ref_2 → novel_2
```

= 6 trials per cell × 3 cells = 18 paired-control warmstart trials.
Each `novel_i` is a fresh LLM-novel kernel emission via T-29's
per-cell call (so emission variance across the 3 replicates is real
LLM variance, not the same kernel re-run).

The post-run analysis groups by cell, computes per-trial Δ% NOV-vs-REF
within each (ref_i, novel_i) pair, and reports:

| Cell | n_pairs | win-rate | mean Δ% | Δ% range | NOV KL range |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| rmsnorm/fp16/large | 3 | …/3 | … | … to … | … to … |
| rmsnorm/bf16/medium | 3 | …/3 | … | … to … | … to … |
| silu_mul/fp16/large | 3 | …/3 | … | … to … | … to … |

A "win" = NOV tok/s ≥ REF tok/s × 1.03 with NOV KL within `gate.max_kl=5.0`.

The cleanest publishable result is: "in 3/3 trials at rmsnorm/fp16/large
the LLM-novel kernel beat the reference by 8–18%." Anything noisier
(e.g. 1/3 wins with high variance) is also publishable but lands as
"effect not robust at N=3, more replication needed."

### Q2 — class-aware feasibility on H100

H100 has no FP8 dead zone, so the classifier doesn't get the
FP8-on-A100 free lunch. The remaining failure modes the classifier
should learn are:
- FP8 + XFORMERS (catalog rule, hardware-independent)
- OOM regions when the H100's 80GB hits gpu_memory_utilization × max_num_seqs
- QUALITY_KL drift on aggressive quantization combos

Measurement:
```
n_warmstart_kept / n_warmstart   = … / 4
n_operator_kept   / n_operator    = … / ~3
n_surrogate_kept  / n_surrogate   = … / ~7
n_reserve_kept    / n_reserve     = … / 0–6  (only fires if L2 stale-fires)
```

Headline: L1 surrogate kept-rate. Target ≥ 30%.

Auxiliary: did the classifier filter any catalog-violation proposals
before subprocess spawn? Pre-spawn rejects appear as `elapsed_s ≈ 1e-4`
in trial JSONs.

### Q3 — cross-hardware comparison

Build a per-cell delta table from C02 (A100, N=1) and C03 (H100, N=3):

| Cell | C02 NOV Δ% (A100, N=1) | C03 mean NOV Δ% (H100, N=3) | Agreement? |
|---|---|---|---|
| rmsnorm/fp16/large | +14.7% | … | … |
| rmsnorm/bf16/medium | −11.13% | … | … |
| silu_mul/fp16/large | +0.77% | … | … |

"Agreement" criterion: signs match AND magnitudes within ±5%. The
campaign cannot answer "do these specific kernels win on a third
hardware class" — that's a future replication. But A100 ↔ H100
agreement at three cells is enough to claim "the LLM-novel kernel's
edge generalizes within the same SM-architecture family."

If the signs **don't** match (e.g. NOV beats REF on A100 but loses on
H100), that's an honestly-publishable nuanced result — kernel novelty
is hardware-class-specific, the methodology footprint is narrower
than the Q1-only writeup would suggest. We'd expand the kernel
proposer's hardware-aware prompting in a follow-up.

### Q4 — T-29 parser reliability

Count `[autoinfer.l3.proposer] paired-control fallback` markers in
the campaign-runner stdout. Target ≤ 1 over the 9 paired observations
(was 2 over 6 in C02). Each marker means an LLM call returned no
parseable block; the cell falls back to its reference (the pair
degenerates to two reference trials at the same cell — still useful
data but doesn't add a NOV observation).

Auxiliary: per-cell parser reliability. If silu_mul still has a
disproportionate fallback rate even with per-cell calls, the LLM is
struggling with silu_mul kernel emission generally, not delimiter
hygiene; opens T-30 for richer silu_mul few-shots or fine-tuning.

## Expected outcomes

### Outcome A — Q1 robust replication at the C02 winner
*Probability:* medium-low

What we'd see at rmsnorm/fp16/large: 3/3 NOV wins with mean Δ% in
+10% to +20% range. The C02 +14.7% is not noise; the LLM-novel rmsnorm
kernel for fp16/large is genuinely better than the PyTorch reference
on H100.

What it answers: Q1 robustly affirmed. Combined with C02's A100
datapoint (+14.7%), Q3 likely also agrees. **The article-grade headline
result.** Triggers a Campaign 04 with replication on a third hardware
class (RTX A6000 or A100-40GB) for full hardware-class coverage.

### Outcome B — Q1 mixed (1/3 or 2/3 wins, high variance)
*Probability:* medium-high

What we'd see at rmsnorm/fp16/large: e.g. one trial +18%, one trial
+2%, one trial −5%. The C02 +14.7% was real but the LLM-novel kernel
emission has high variance — sometimes it produces a strong winner,
sometimes barely a tie.

What it answers: Q1 partially affirmed. Worth publishing as "in 1-of-3
to 2-of-3 trials, the LLM-novel rmsnorm kernel for fp16/large beats
the reference by N%; effect size is real but emission variance is
high." Opens T-31 for kernel-emission-quality measurement (perplexity-
style scoring of the LLM's kernel proposals before serving).

### Outcome C — Q1 negative (0/3 NOV wins)
*Probability:* low-medium

What we'd see: all 3 NOV trials within ±2% of REF, or all worse. The
C02 +14.7% was lucky single-shot emission noise.

What it answers: Q1 honestly negative at this cell. The LLM-novel
kernel architecture works (correctness gate passes) but doesn't
reliably outperform vLLM's reference on rmsnorm/fp16/large. Could
still be cell-specific (some cells genuinely have headroom; others
don't). Triggers Campaign 04 either expanding to more cells or moving
to T-21 (attention injector).

### Outcome D — Q2 affirmed on H100
*Probability:* medium

What we'd see: L1 surrogate kept-rate ≥ 30%. The classifier learns
H100-specific failure modes (FP8 + XFORMERS, OOM regions) within the
12-trial budget.

### Outcome E — Q2 partial / negative on H100
*Probability:* medium

What we'd see: surrogate kept-rate 15-25%. T-26b's weights help

deterministic-failure regions than A100 (no FP8 dead zone), so the

softer (OOM, KL drift) and don't generalize cleanly through the k-NN
distance.

If this lands, opens T-26c for per-FailureKind sub-classifiers OR a
finer-grained surrogate-feedback loop.

### Outcome F — Q3 cross-hardware agreement
*Probability:* medium

C02 (A100) and C03 (H100) deltas agree in sign and within ±5% magnitude

### Outcome H — T-29 fixes parser brittleness
*Probability:* high

### Outcome I — Integration breakage
*Probability:* low (T-26b + T-29 + paired-control extensions all

## Q1 corrective audit v2 (2026-04-30) — kernel-level vs PyTorch unfused

> **Reading note:** This section is the *first* corrective audit. The
> Sonnet-vs-PyTorch-unfused numbers below remain correct, but the
> framing ("2–9× faster than the PyTorch reference") was a
> strawman comparison: production vLLM does not run unfused PyTorch.
> See the **v3 three-way audit** section below for the production-
> relevant answer (vLLM-native CUDA is faster than Sonnet's Triton at
> every shape).

After the original Q1 conclusion was challenged, a kernel-level
microbench was deployed on a fresh 1× A100 80GB PCIe to time the SAME
9 Sonnet 4 NOV kernels from C03-S against the PyTorch reference, in
isolation. This addresses the methodology gap: end-to-end serving
tok/s cannot expose 2-9× kernel-level speedups when the kernel is
~5-10% of compute and measurement noise is ~0.5-1%.

### Audit setup

- **Hardware:** 1× NVIDIA A100 80GB PCIe (Hyperstack on-demand,
  separate Basilica deployment from C03-S)
- **Software:** torch 2.11+cu130, triton 3.6.0 (pre-installed in
  `vllm/vllm-openai:latest`)
- **Sources:** the same 9 NOV kernel sources from C03-S trial JSONs
  (extracted by SHA, no modifications)
- **Reference:** the same PyTorch unfused kernel C03-S used as REF
  (`x.to(fp32) → mean(sq) → rsqrt → mul w → cast back`)
- **Timer:** 20 warmup iterations + 50 measure iterations per shape
  via `torch.cuda.Event` with explicit `synchronize` between every
  measurement. Reports median + IQR (p25, p75). Median is the
  headline (best-of-K is biased by clock-boost / cache-warm).
- **Shapes:** the cell shape from each NOV's C03-S regime (e.g.
  128×8192 for "large") + 5 Qwen3-8B production shapes:
  decode_b1 (1×4096), decode_b32 (32×4096), prefill_1k (1024×4096),
  prefill_4k (4096×4096), prefill_16k (16384×4096) for rmsnorm; same
  pattern with 22016 last-dim for silu_mul.
- **Correctness:** atol=1e-2, rtol=1e-2 (looser than C03-S's 1e-3
  to allow Triton's accumulation-order drift on fp16/bf16; same op
  invariants).

### Audit results — rmsnorm (6 NOV implementations)

All 6 NOV rmsnorm implementations (3 fp16/large, 3 bf16/medium):

```
correctness:  ALL PASS (max_abs_err ≤ 0.002, n_violating = 0)
```

| Shape | N | Median speedup_x (NOV vs REF) | Range |
|---|---|---|---|
| 128×8192 (cell)        | 6 | **2.78×** | [1.93x .. 3.13x] |
| 32×4096 (cell)         | 6 | **2.80×** | [2.20x .. 2.86x] |
| 1×4096 (decode_b1)     | 6 | **2.72×** | [2.08x .. 3.49x] |
| 32×4096 (decode_b32)   | 6 | **2.74×** | [1.94x .. 2.93x] |
| 1024×4096 (prefill_1k) | 6 | **3.28×** | [1.84x .. 3.41x] |
| 4096×4096 (prefill_4k) | 6 | **6.86×** | [5.03x .. 7.04x] |
| 16384×4096 (prefill_16k)| 6 | **9.39×** | [9.08x .. 9.50x] |

**Reading:** Sonnet 4's rmsnorm kernels are 2-9× faster than the
PyTorch reference at the kernel level, consistently across 6
independent emissions. The speedup grows with shape (textbook
memory-bandwidth-bound: PyTorch unfused does 5+ kernel launches
with multiple memory passes; Sonnet's fused Triton does 1 pass).

**Interpretation of C03-S end-to-end ties for rmsnorm pairs:**
With rmsnorm at ~5% of Qwen3-8B compute and ~3-5× kernel speedup
(realistic decode-shape range), expected end-to-end gain is ~3-4%.
C03-S noise floor is ±0.5-1%; expected Δ% would be barely visible
above noise, possibly ~+1% to +3%. Observed: −0.20% mean. The
direction (slight slowdown not slight gain) is itself worth
investigating — possible Inductor / cudagraph / monkey-patch
overhead negating the kernel-level gain. **Not currently
explained.**

### Audit results — silu_mul (3 NOV implementations)

Sonnet 4's silu_mul kernels are **degraded**:

| Trial | Shape | speedup_x | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| `w0013` (C03-S startup-failed) | (all shapes) | n/a — Triton CompilationError | The Triton kernel itself doesn't compile. Confirms the C03-S `outcome=startup` was a real Triton-side bug, not a vLLM issue. |
| `w0015` | 128×8192 | **0.01×** (100× SLOWER) | NOV uses `torch.cat([x_flat, y_flat])` allocating 2× memory for nothing; massively memory-bound |
| `w0017` | 128×8192 | 0.32× (3× SLOWER) | Same `torch.cat` anti-pattern, less catastrophic |

**Yet C03-S end-to-end showed silu_mul NOV pairs as ties (+0.10%
mean).** If the patched silu_mul is 3-100× slower at the kernel
level and silu_mul is ~3-5% of compute, end-to-end should be
0.5-15% slower. Observed delta of essentially zero strongly
suggests:

> **vLLM's MLP path does NOT actually call our patched
> `SiluAndMul.forward_cuda` during serving.**

Possible explanations:
1. vLLM's Qwen3 MLP uses a fused `gemm + silu_mul` path that
   bypasses `SiluAndMul.forward_cuda` entirely
2. Inductor compilation specializes the MLP and substitutes
   our patched kernel out
3. vLLM's CustomOp dispatcher routes to `forward_native` (a
   different entry point) for serving, not `forward_cuda`

This is **a separate bug** — the L3 paired-control protocol's
silu_mul-side measurements were not actually testing the patched
kernel during C03-S serving. Needs verification before any future
silu_mul claim.

### Corrective Q1 reading

The original "Q1 NEGATIVE" claim is **retracted**. The corrected
reading per the kernel-level evidence:

1. **Sonnet 4 emits meaningfully faster rmsnorm kernels (2-9×) at
   the kernel level.** This is the actual kernel-novelty answer;
   the architecture (LLM proposes Triton kernels, gates pass,
   monkey-patch into vLLM, end-to-end serves) does work for
   rmsnorm — the kernel itself is fast.
2. **End-to-end serving on Qwen3-8B is the wrong measurement
   surface for this kernel.** rmsnorm is too small a fraction of
   compute (~3-5% on this model) for kernel-level wins to clear
   the ±0.5-1% measurement noise. Even the C01-doc analysis I
   wrote — "rmsnorm and silu_mul are tiny fractions of total
   compute on Qwen3-8B; kernel speedup translates to ~0.5-2%
   end-to-end" — was already this exact diagnosis, which I lost
   sight of when designing C03's paired-control around end-to-end
   tok/s.
3. **The C02 +14.7% rmsnorm/fp16/large NOV win** is now hard to
   reconcile cleanly. The kernel-level speedup is ~3× at that
   shape; the maximum end-to-end translation is ~3-5%. C02's
   +14.7% is far above that ceiling — likely a single-shot
   warmup-or-batch-scheduling fluke. Not "the win replicates"
   but also not "C03 falsifies the kernel-novelty claim."
4. **silu_mul measurement is broken** — separate verification
   needed before any silu_mul-related claim.

### What this means for the next campaign

- **End-to-end paired-control on rmsnorm/silu_mul is a dead end.**
  Replace it with kernel-level micro-A/B (the audit pattern run
  here) for any future "does the LLM-novel kernel beat reference"
  question on minor ops.
- **For end-to-end Q1 wins, move to attention (T-21).** Attention
  is ~70% of Qwen3-8B compute; a 1.2-1.5× attention kernel
  speedup would yield ~10-25% end-to-end gain, well above noise.
- **Verify the silu_mul monkey-patch is actually called during
  serving** before any future silu_mul-related kernel claim.

## Q1 corrective audit v3 (2026-04-30) — three-way vs vLLM-native CUDA

**The production-relevant kernel-novelty speedup at the rmsnorm
surface on Qwen3-8B + A100 is NEGATIVE.** Replacing vLLM's CUDA
rmsnorm with Sonnet 4's single-shot Triton emission would slow
inference by 6–65% at this kernel, with the largest slowdowns at
the small/decode shapes that dominate real serving workloads.

This does not invalidate the framework — the C03-S architecture
(LLM emits Triton, gates pass, monkey-patch into vLLM, end-to-end
serves) demonstrably works end-to-end. What it falsifies is the
specific hypothesis that **a single-shot LLM emission of a Triton
rmsnorm kernel beats the production CUDA baseline**. Beating
vLLM-native CUDA is genuinely hard; the C03-S Sonnet emissions
beat unfused PyTorch but do not clear the production bar.

### Implications for the next campaign

- **Do not cite "kernel speedup" without a production baseline.**
  The actual production baseline is `vllm._custom_ops.*`, not
  `torch unfused`. Any future kernel-level audit must use the
  three-way primitive (PyTorch / vllm-native / NOV) so the
  production-relevant column is reported alongside the kernel-
  quality-floor column.
- **End-to-end paired-control on rmsnorm/silu_mul is doubly a
  dead end** — both because rmsnorm is too small a fraction of
  Qwen3-8B compute (the v2 finding) AND because Sonnet's Triton
  doesn't beat the production CUDA baseline at the kernel level
  in the first place (the v3 finding).
- **For genuine kernel-novelty Q1 wins, options are (in
  priority order):**
  1. Move to attention (T-21): attention is ~70% of compute;
     vLLM's attention backends (FlashAttention, FlashInfer) are
     already highly optimised but the surface is large enough that
     a single-shot LLM emission has more degrees of freedom.
  2. Try a stronger code-emission model (Sonnet 4 → Sonnet 4.5,
     GPT-5-codex, DeepSeek-Coder-33B) and re-run the rmsnorm
     three-way.
  3. Add post-emission autotuning (Triton autotune sweep over
     `BLOCK_SIZE`, `num_warps`, `num_stages`) to convert single-shot
     emissions into autotuned candidates before the production
     baseline comparison. Single-shot Triton at Triton's worst
     defaults loses to a hand-tuned CUDA; an autotuned Triton may
     close the gap.
  4. Target less-optimised kernel surfaces — vLLM's `silu_and_mul`
     is in-tree CUDA but other auxiliary ops (rotary, layernorm-
     variant, score-mod ops) may have less aggressive baselines
     on certain hardware classes.
- **Open T-32:** Sonnet's Triton kernels lose to vLLM-native at the
  rmsnorm surface; production-relevant kernel-novelty claim falsified
  on this op×model×hardware combination. Methodology must require
  three-way (PyTorch / vllm-native / NOV) for any future kernel claim.
